# Colombia's Education System: A Nation Rewriting Its Future, One Classroom at a Time

*From colonial church schools to free university tuition — how 35 million students became the heart of a peace project*

---

Somewhere in the mountains of Nariño, a teacher named Luz Elena wakes before sunrise. She will walk 90 minutes on a dirt path, cross a stream on a wooden plank bridge, and arrive at a multi-grade classroom where she is simultaneously the science teacher, the reading coach, and the school counselor for children spanning six grade levels. She earns roughly COP 2,700,000 a month — about $650. She has not missed a day in three years.

Across the country in Bogotá, a 19-year-old named Sebastián — first in his family to ever sit in a university classroom — is registering for his second semester of Systems Engineering at a public university. His tuition bill: zero pesos. That was not possible for his older brother, who dropped out at 16.

These two images — the underpaid rural teacher and the first-generation university student — define the paradox and the promise of education in Colombia today. This is a system that has accomplished things that would have seemed fantastical just 30 years ago, and yet remains fractured in ways that cannot be fixed with policy papers alone.

This is the story of how one of Latin America's most complex nations is trying to educate its way out of inequality, conflict, and geography.

## 1. Why Colombia's Education System Matters Right Now {#why-now}

Colombia became the 37th member of the OECD on April 28, 2020 — a milestone that placed its schools, universities, and teachers under a microscope unlike anything the country had experienced before. Suddenly, Colombian classrooms were being measured not against neighbors like Venezuela or Ecuador, but against Finland, South Korea, and Germany.

The timing was brutal. Six months later, COVID-19 shuttered every school in the country, and millions of children vanished from the education system overnight — not because of the virus, but because they had no internet, no device, and in some cases, no electricity.

And yet, by 2025, Colombia was doing something remarkable: pushing through the most ambitious higher education funding reform in the country's history, absorbing over 550,000 Venezuelan migrant children into public schools, and rolling out free university tuition for the majority of its population.

**The story of Colombian education is not a success story, and it is not a failure story. It is something far more interesting: a real-time transformation, still in progress.**

If you are a researcher, a policymaker, a parent, or simply someone who cares about what happens when a nation tries to reinvent itself through learning — Colombia in 2025 is the most important case study on the planet.

## 2. From Colonizers to Constitution: 500 Years of Education History {#history}

### Before the Spanish Arrived

The territory now called Colombia was home to some of the most sophisticated civilizations in the hemisphere. The Muisca of the central highlands, the Tairona in the Sierra Nevada, the Quimbaya in the central cordillera — none of them built universities, but all of them educated their children with precision and purpose.

Their classrooms were the forest, the field, and the ceremonial fire. Elders and shamans transmitted agricultural science, astronomy accurate enough to track planting seasons, metallurgy sophisticated enough to create the gold objects now displayed in the world's great museums, and a web of social knowledge that held entire civilizations together. It was education without paper, but it was not education without rigor.

The Spanish arrived in the 16th century and dismantled all of it.

### The Church Takes Over (1550–1810)

The Dominican, Jesuit, and Franciscan orders built the first formal schools — and built them exclusively for the criollo elite and the colonial clergy. The curriculum was Latin, theology, scholastic philosophy, and law. For indigenous and mestizo populations, "education" meant religious catechism and the vocational training needed for manual labor on the encomiendas.

Universities like the Universidad de Santo Tomás (founded 1580), the Colegio Mayor de San Bartolomé (1604), and the Universidad del Rosario (1653) were not places of universal knowledge. They were instruments of colonial control.

### The Great Secular Fight (1810–1900)

Independence from Spain in 1810 did not liberate Colombian education overnight. What followed was a century of ferocious ideological warfare between Liberals — who wanted compulsory, secular, state-run schools — and Conservatives — who insisted the Catholic Church maintain its monopoly on minds.

Into this battle stepped Francisco de Paula Santander, nicknamed "The Organizer of Victory." Santander believed, with almost evangelical conviction, that a republic of ignorant citizens was a republic doomed to fail. He imported the Lancasterian method — a brilliant, resource-poor system where older students taught younger ones, allowing a single teacher to educate hundreds — and set about trying to build a national school system from almost nothing.

The Organic Decree of Public Instruction in 1870 was the Liberal high-water mark: a genuine attempt to create a secular, German-influenced national primary school system. The Church fought it. Conservative governments reversed it. Civil wars interrupted it.

Colombia's 19th century was a lesson in how hard it is to build an education system when the country itself is tearing apart.

### From Law to Life: The 20th Century Builds a System

Law 39 of 1903 finally categorized education into distinct tiers: primary, secondary, professional. The Liberal Republic of the 1930s, under President Alfonso López Pumarejo's "Revolution on the March," pushed hard to expand public access and modernize the Universidad Nacional.

In 1957, a milestone that still resonates today: the founding of the **National Learning Service (SENA)**, a vocational training institution that would eventually become one of the largest and most respected of its kind in the world.

And then, in 1991, everything changed.

### The Constitution That Rewrote the Rules

The 1991 Political Constitution was not just a legal document. It was a national reckoning. Written in the shadow of drug cartel violence, guerrilla warfare, and social collapse, it declared education a fundamental human right — not a privilege, not a gift, but a right.

That declaration cascaded into the **General Law of Education (Law 115 of 1994)**, which decentralized the system, gave schools autonomy over their pedagogical identity, and created the framework for everything that followed. **Law 30 of 1992** had already restructured higher education and established university autonomy.

These three documents — the Constitution, Law 115, and Law 30 — are the architecture on which modern Colombian education stands.

## 3. How the System Is Governed — And Who Really Runs the Schools {#governance}

### The Three-Level Architecture

Colombian education is governed through a deliberately decentralized structure that can confuse outsiders and occasionally confuses insiders too.

At the national level, the **Ministry of National Education (MEN)** sets macro-policy, creates curriculum standards, manages the national budget transfers, and oversees quality. Think of it as the architect.

Below that are **97 Certified Territorial Entities (ETCs)** — all 32 departments, major cities like Bogotá, Medellín, and Cali, and qualifying municipalities. These ETCs are the builders. They hire and pay teachers, maintain school buildings, manage the school feeding program, and run the day-to-day machinery of education for their jurisdictions.

At the bottom — which is actually where everything real happens — is the individual school, governed through what the law calls the **Gobierno Escolar** (School Government): a democratically structured body including the principal, teachers, parents, students, and community representatives.

Every school in Colombia is legally required to have its own **Proyecto Educativo Institucional (PEI)** — essentially its educational constitution. This document defines the school's pedagogical philosophy, community mission, and curriculum approach. A rural school in Chocó and a private bilingual academy in Bogotá are both required to follow national standards, but their PEIs can look completely different.

This is either the system's greatest strength or its greatest weakness, depending on what day you ask and whose school you're visiting.

### Quality Watchdogs

Two specialized bodies maintain system integrity: the **Colombian Institute for the Evaluation of Education (ICFES)**, which designs and administers all national standardized tests, and the **National Accreditation Council (CNA)**, which handles voluntary but increasingly essential university accreditation.

For long-term strategy beyond electoral cycles, the **National Decennial Education Plan 2016–2026** serves as a 10-year blueprint — a deliberate attempt to insulate educational progress from the whims of four-year governments.

## 4. Follow the Money: How Education Is Funded in Colombia (2025 Update) {#funding}

### The Big Numbers

Colombia invests approximately **4.5% of GDP** in education — roughly **17.2% of the total national government budget** as of 2023. That places it in the mid-range among OECD members, a respectable position for a middle-income country still managing significant internal conflicts.

Public funding accounts for about **82%** of total education expenditure. The remaining 18% comes from households — out-of-pocket costs for uniforms, materials, transport, and private tutoring that quietly punish the poor.

The funding pipeline flows primarily through the **Sistema General de Participaciones (SGP)**, which transfers national revenue to regional ETCs to cover teacher salaries, infrastructure, and services. For higher education, the central government makes direct transfers to public universities under Law 30 of 1992.

### The Matrícula Cero Revolution

In 2021, against the backdrop of pandemic-era economic devastation, Colombia institutionalized **Matrícula Cero** — free undergraduate tuition for students in socioeconomic strata 1, 2, and 3 at public higher education institutions.

The impact was immediate and enormous. Students who would have faced fees ranging from COP 6,000,000 to COP 18,000,000 per semester at private institutions, or proportional fees at public ones, suddenly had a path to university that didn't require taking on crushing debt or abandoning their families' economic survival.

### 🔴 Update (December 2025): The Biggest Higher Education Reform in Decades

Here is something that happened after most international coverage of Colombian education stopped paying attention, and it may be the single most important development in the system in a generation.

On **December 15, 2025**, Colombia's Chamber of Representatives voted unanimously — **116 votes, zero against** — to reform Articles 86 and 87 of Law 30 of 1992, the foundational legislation governing public university funding.

The change sounds technical. The impact is seismic.

Previously, funding for public universities was adjusted annually using the **Consumer Price Index (CPI)** — essentially tied to general inflation. This meant that as university operating costs (laboratory equipment, faculty salaries, research infrastructure) grew faster than consumer inflation, universities were perpetually starved of resources. They could enroll more students but couldn't actually afford to educate them well.

The new law ties funding to the **Higher Education Cost Index**, which tracks actual university operating costs. It's the difference between measuring a hospital's budget by the price of groceries versus the price of medical equipment.

The scale of what has already changed is striking:

> Funding for public higher education rose from **5.5 trillion pesos to nearly 12 trillion pesos in 2025** — more than double in three years. In 2026, it will exceed **13 trillion pesos**, without raising taxes.

This is not an abstraction. It means more professors, better laboratories, more student support services, and — critically — the financial sustainability of Matrícula Cero, which the student movements of 2018–2019 always warned was being implemented without the institutional money to back it up.

The 2018–2019 student protests, which paralyzed Colombian campuses for months and brought tens of thousands of students into the streets, are now visible in retrospect as the turning point that made this possible. They demanded historic agreements. They got them — eventually, and larger than anyone anticipated.

### What Families Still Pay

Despite free public options, household education costs remain real:

- **Private primary and secondary tuition**: COP 600,000 to COP 2,500,000+ per month
- **Private higher education**: COP 6,000,000 to COP 18,000,000 per semester
- **Basic out-of-pocket costs** (uniforms, materials, transport) even in free public schools: estimated at 3%–7% of average household consumption

For a family in the bottom income quintile, that 3%–7% is not a minor inconvenience. It's a monthly decision.

### The Shadow Education Economy

In 2019, a UNESCO study found that approximately **35% of urban secondary students** were enrolled in private tutoring — not because they wanted enrichment, but because the Saber 11 exam determines university access, and competing for spots at the best public universities means competing against students whose families pay COP 30,000 to COP 100,000 per hour for coaching.

Think about what that means in human terms: a student from a low-income family, preparing for the most important exam of their life, staying up past midnight working through practice problems alone, competing against a classmate who spent Saturday morning with a private math tutor in Chapinero.

The exam is the same. The preparation is not. This is the invisible inequality that no tuition policy can fix alone.

## 5. The Structure: From Cradle to Doctorate {#structure}

Colombia's formal education system is sequential, structured, and more complex than it initially appears.

### Early Childhood (Ages 0–6)
Not compulsory, but heavily subsidized for vulnerable populations through the Colombian Institute of Family Welfare (ICBF). Programs range from community-based Madres Comunitarias to full Centros de Desarrollo Infantil. In 2022, only about **45% of children aged 3–5** were enrolled in formal early childhood programs — a gap that compounds into academic disadvantage that follows children for the rest of their educational lives.

### Pre-school: Three Grades, One Mandatory
Three grades: Pre-jardín (age 3), Jardín (age 4), and Transición (age 5). Only **Transición is compulsory** and must be offered free by public schools.

### Basic Education (Ages 6–14) — The Constitutional Core
This is the heart of the mandatory system: five years of Basic Primary (Grades 1–5) followed by four years of Basic Secondary (Grades 6–9). Completing Grade 9 satisfies the minimum constitutional requirement.

### Upper Secondary (Grades 10–11) — The Great Bifurcation
Not technically mandatory, but practically essential for any formal employment or university access. Students choose between:

- **Media Académica**: Deep preparation in sciences, humanities, mathematics, and philosophy for university
- **Media Técnica**: Vocational specialization, often delivered in partnership with SENA, leading to a double certification — a bachiller diploma plus a SENA technical certificate — that can mean immediate entry into the labor market

Completion of Grade 11 earns the **Bachiller** title and requires passing the Saber 11 national exam.

### Higher Education: The Four-Level Tertiary Pyramid
Under Law 30 of 1992:
- **Technical Professional** (1.5–2 years)
- **Technological** (2.5–3 years)
- **Professional University Degree** (4–5 years, conferring protected titles like Engineer, Doctor, Lawyer)
- **Postgraduate**: Specializations (1 year), Master's degrees (2 years), and Doctorates (3–5 years)

A unique feature of Colombian higher education is **Ciclos Propedéuticos** — a ladder structure that allows students who completed technical or technological programs to "step up" to a full professional degree without starting over, recognizing their prior learning.

### For Those Who Fell Through the Cracks: Adult Education
The CLEI system (Ciclos Lectivos Especiales Integrados) compresses grades 1–11 into six accelerated cycles, allowing adults to complete basic and secondary education and earn their Bachiller. It is an acknowledgment that for millions of Colombians, life interrupted school — and that it's never too late to return.

### The Calendar Divide
**Calendar A** (used by ~90% of schools): January/February to November, aligned with Colombia's domestic rhythms.

**Calendar B** (elite private international schools): August/September to June, synchronized with North American and European academic schedules to facilitate international university admissions.

The calendar you attend often signals the zip code you come from.

## 6. What Students Actually Learn: Curriculum and Pedagogy {#curriculum}

### No Single National Textbook — By Design

Colombia deliberately does not impose a rigid national syllabus. Instead, the MEN provides **Estándares Básicos de Competencias (EBC)** — the baseline competency standards every student must meet — and **Derechos Básicos de Aprendizaje (DBA)** — a year-by-year map of what every child has the right to learn.

Within those guardrails, schools design their own curriculum through their PEI. The goal is that a school in Guainía can teach the same mathematics standards as one in Chapinero while doing so in ways that connect to the Amazon rainforest community around it.

The mandatory core areas (which must account for at least **80% of instructional time**) include: natural sciences, social sciences and history, arts, ethics and human values, physical education, religious education (with opt-out rights), Spanish and literature, foreign languages, mathematics, and technology and informatics.

### Bilingualism: The Ambition and the Gap

English is mandated as the primary foreign language. The **National Bilingual Program** aims to align Colombian students with the Common European Framework of Reference (CEFR). In practice, English teaching quality varies enormously — from certified bilingual teachers at elite private schools to public school educators who themselves learned English from outdated textbooks.

### The Rural Innovation That Colombia Gave the World

In the 1970s, Colombia invented **Escuela Nueva** — a pedagogical model for multi-grade rural classrooms, where a single teacher must simultaneously teach children spanning multiple grades. Using self-paced learning guides, flexible promotion (a student can move to the next level when ready, not when the calendar says so), and curriculum that integrates the agricultural and cultural reality of rural communities, Escuela Nueva became globally recognized. UNESCO has cited it as a model worth replicating worldwide.

It is, in many ways, Colombia's greatest pedagogical export — born not from abundance, but from the necessity of trying to educate children in places the state could barely reach.

### The Cátedra de la Paz: Teaching Peace as a Subject

Mandated by law in 2014, the **Cátedra de la Paz** is one of the most uniquely Colombian features of the national curriculum. It is not simply a civics class. It is a structured, cross-disciplinary subject weaving through social sciences, ethics, and humanities — designed to confront the historical memory of five decades of internal armed conflict and build a culture of non-violent coexistence from the ground up.

For a generation of children born after the 2016 FARC peace deal, the Cátedra de la Paz is the curriculum space where they encounter — often for the first time — the story of the war their parents lived through.

### The Push for Jornada Única

Colombia has been aggressively transitioning from a split-shift model (where schools ran morning and afternoon sessions for different student groups, resulting in only 4–5 hours of instruction per day) to the **Jornada Única** — a full 8-hour school day. The extended day allows for guided study time, arts enrichment, physical education, and intensive academic reinforcement inside school hours, rather than pushing everything onto homework that students in resource-poor homes often cannot complete.

## 7. The Exams That Define a Life: Saber 11 and Beyond {#assessment}

### The Architecture of Accountability

The **ICFES** (Colombian Institute for the Evaluation of Education) runs one of Latin America's most comprehensive national testing systems. The suite of standardized **Pruebas Saber** tracks students from primary school through university graduation.

- **Saber 3°, 5°, 7°, 9°**: Diagnostic exams in language, math, science, and citizenship. These don't affect grades — they feed data to the system to identify where learning is breaking down before it becomes irreversible.

- **Saber 11°**: The high-stakes exit exam at the end of Grade 11, testing Critical Reading, Mathematics, Social and Citizen Competencies, Natural Sciences, and English. Your score on this exam is, for the vast majority of students, your entire higher education admission portfolio. Getting into the Universidad Nacional de Colombia or receiving a Generación E scholarship doesn't hinge on your four years of effort in secondary school — it hinges significantly on one day's performance on the Saber 11.

- **Saber Pro**: Administered to university students who have completed 75% of their degree, evaluating both generic competencies (critical reading, quantitative reasoning, written communication, English) and field-specific knowledge. This is Colombia's mechanism for holding universities accountable for what they actually teach.

- **Saber TyT**: The parallel exam for technical and technological graduates.

### Classroom Evaluation: The SIEE System

Inside schools, evaluation is governed by Decree 1290 of 2009, which requires every institution to design its own **Sistema Institucional de Evaluación de los Estudiantes (SIEE)**. The philosophy is competency-based: evaluation must measure what students can do with knowledge, not just whether they can recall facts.

Performance is classified into four tiers: **Superior, Alto, Básico** (minimum passing), and **Bajo** (failing). Self-evaluation and peer-evaluation are mandated — Colombian students are legally required to assess their own learning.

### Accommodation and Inclusion

Under Decree 1421 of 2017, ICFES is legally obligated to provide accommodations for students with disabilities: extended time, Braille materials, sign language interpreters, assistive software. The principle is that national exams must measure cognitive competency, not the absence of sight.

## 8. The Teachers: A Profession Divided Against Itself {#teachers}

### Two Statutes, One Staffroom

The single most confusing, contentious, and consequential structural feature of the Colombian teaching workforce is its **dual legal regime**.

**Decree 2277 of 1979** covers teachers hired before 2002. Career advancement is based primarily on seniority and academic credentials. Job security is nearly absolute — removal requires severe misconduct. Periodic performance evaluations that affect retention? Not required.

**Decree 1278 of 2002** governs everyone hired after June 2002. To enter the profession, candidates must pass a competitive civil service exam. Annual performance evaluations by their rector are mandatory. Two consecutive evaluation scores below 60%? Dismissal. Teachers under this statute sometimes describe it as walking a tightrope while their veteran colleagues sit comfortably in chairs.

The two groups share school buildings, staff meetings, and lunch tables, but live in completely different professional realities. A teacher hired in 2001 has near-total security. A teacher hired in 2003 faces a lifetime of evaluations. As of early 2025, the Ministry of Education and FECODE (the powerful national teachers' union) were actively negotiating a **Single Statute** that would harmonize these conditions — a reform that both sides describe as long overdue and both define completely differently.

### Who Teaches Colombia's Children

The public basic and secondary workforce numbers approximately **330,000 teachers** serving over 8 million students. About **65–70% are women**, a concentration that is most pronounced in early childhood and primary education.

Teachers enter through two main pathways:
1. **Escuelas Normales Superiores (ENS)** — specialized secondary-to-tertiary institutions producing "Normalistas" qualified to teach at early childhood and primary levels, vital in rural areas universities don't reach
2. **University Licenciatura degrees** — 5-year professional programs combining disciplinary depth with pedagogical training

### What Teachers Earn

A new public school teacher with an undergraduate degree earns approximately **COP 2,700,000 per month** — roughly $650 USD. A highly experienced teacher with a master's or doctoral degree at the top of the salary scale can earn upward of **COP 5,500,000**.

These are respectable numbers relative to other regional public sector roles with similar credentials. They are not respectable numbers compared to what a 5-year university graduate can earn in the private sector, which is why the profession struggles to attract and retain talent — particularly in mathematics, physics, and the natural sciences.

### The Hidden Heroes: Teachers in Red Zones

In territories historically controlled by guerrilla groups, paramilitary organizations, or criminal networks, teachers face something no evaluation rubric captures: threats, extortion, and forced displacement. In isolated municipalities, the local teacher is frequently the only representative of the Colombian state present in the community — part educator, part social worker, part proof that the government hasn't forgotten these people exist.

FECODE negotiates not only salary scales but specialized healthcare and security provisions for these educators. It is one of the most politically powerful unions in Latin America, capable of shutting down national education with a strike authorization.

### The Todos a Aprender Program

The government's flagship teacher development initiative deploys expert educators as in-school coaches — not to run workshops, but to sit beside teachers in their own classrooms, model effective lessons, and build pedagogical skill through direct, situated collaboration. It is expensive. It is slow. And the evidence suggests it works.

## 9. The Equity Crisis: Who Gets Left Behind {#equity}

### The Numbers Underneath the Headline

Colombia's headline enrollment statistics look impressive. In 2022:
- Net primary enrollment: **93.9%**
- Lower secondary enrollment: **83.5%**
- Upper secondary enrollment: **49.3%** — meaning roughly one in every two Colombian teenagers aged 15–17 is not in school

That last number is the one that should stop you cold. Colombia is not failing to get children into primary school. It is failing, dramatically, to keep them through the educational pipeline.

### 🔴 A Metric That Rarely Makes Headlines: The NEET Rate

Here is a number that deserves far more attention than it receives.

In 2024, **27.4% of Colombians aged 18–24** were classified as NEET — **Neither in Employment nor in Education or Training**. That ranked Colombia **3rd out of 37 OECD and partner countries** with available data. More than one in four young adults — not studying, not working, not training.

This is not just an education statistic. It is a social stability statistic. It is a security statistic. It is the number that explains why families in rural Colombia sometimes regard school as a luxury they cannot afford, and why the FARC and criminal organizations have historically found a ready supply of recruits.

### The Urban-Rural Chasm

No inequality in Colombian education is as well-documented or as stubbornly persistent as the gap between urban and rural outcomes.

Colombia's geography is not a metaphor — it is a daily operational crisis. Dense jungle, high-altitude Andean passes, isolated coastal plains, and riverine communities that are weeks from the nearest city by boat make educational delivery genuinely, physically difficult.

In 2022, rural upper secondary completion rates were estimated at **20–30 percentage points lower** than urban averages. Rural schools frequently have:
- No digital connectivity
- Dilapidated or non-existent infrastructure
- Multi-grade classrooms taught by a single teacher (hence Escuela Nueva's continued importance)
- Chronic difficulty attracting and retaining qualified, specialized educators

The most qualified teachers in Colombia are overwhelmingly concentrated in Bogotá, Medellín, and Cali. The teachers who get the hardest jobs, the most isolated communities, and the fewest professional development resources are almost always the least experienced.

### Ethnicity and the Curriculum Gap

Only approximately **20% of Indigenous youth** successfully completed upper secondary education in 2022. The issues are intersecting: geographic isolation, economic necessity, the cultural disconnect between a standardized Spanish-language curriculum and communities whose knowledge systems are oral, multilingual, and land-based, and the severe shortage of trained bilingual indigenous educators.

While Colombia legally mandates **etnoeducación** — curriculum delivered in native languages and integrating indigenous cosmologies — the practical coverage falls dramatically short of the legal ambition.

### 550,000 Children from Venezuela

By early 2024, the Colombian public school system had absorbed approximately **550,000 Venezuelan children and adolescents** — a migration response that is, by almost any measure, one of the most remarkable humanitarian education achievements of recent years.

Colombia's policy guarantees educational access regardless of legal migratory status. Children can enroll with a Temporary Protection Permit. SIMAT (the enrollment system) was modified to register undocumented students. The commitment, at the policy level, is genuine.

The implementation is strained. Schools in border departments like Norte de Santander are overcrowded. Psychosocial support for traumatized migrant children is inadequate. Xenophobia in classrooms is documented. Despite enormous state effort, an estimated **10–15% of school-aged Venezuelan children** remained outside the educational system in 2023.

### Disability: Placement Is Not Inclusion

Following Decree 1421 of 2017, approximately **85% of students with disabilities** were successfully mainstreamed into ordinary public schools by 2022. This is a genuine achievement.

But physical placement is not educational inclusion. The majority of public schools lack the architectural adaptations, specialized pedagogical staff, and assistive technologies needed to implement the mandated Individual Plans of Reasonable Adjustments (PIARs). Students with disabilities experience disproportionately high rates of grade repetition, absenteeism, and dropout — not because of their disabilities, but because of the system's failure to support them.

## 10. Where Colombia Stands Globally: PISA 2022 and the OECD Reckoning {#global}

### The Uncomfortable Numbers

As an OECD member, Colombia participates in PISA — the Programme for International Student Assessment, which measures the math, reading, and science skills of 15-year-olds every three years.

The 2022 results require honest engagement:

- **Mathematics**: Colombian students scored **383 points**, compared to the OECD average of **472 points** — a gap of nearly 90 points, representing roughly two to three years of learning
- **Reading**: Colombia scored **409 points**, against an OECD average of **476 points**

These are not numbers to explain away. They document a genuine, large gap between what Colombian 15-year-olds know and can do, and what 15-year-olds in high-performing OECD countries know and can do. For a country that declared in 2015 that it wanted to be "the most educated country in Latin America by 2025," they are a hard measure of how far that ambition still has to travel.

### The Positive Long-Term Trend — Which Also Matters

Here is the part that gets less attention: Colombia is one of only a handful of countries — alongside Macao, Peru, and Qatar — that has **improved in all three PISA subjects** since it began participating. The trajectory is upward, even if the absolute position is still far below the OECD average.

This is not a contradiction to dismiss. It is a reality to hold alongside the uncomfortable scores: a system moving in the right direction, but moving slowly, from a far starting point, toward a very high bar.

The honest accounting of Colombian education requires holding both truths simultaneously — long-term improvement and present-day underperformance. Claiming only one is ideology. Acknowledging both is analysis.

### Upper Secondary Enrollment: The OECD Comparison

The net enrollment rate among 15–19-year-olds in upper secondary programs in Colombia is **30.5%**, ranking **47th out of 47 countries** with available 2023 OECD data — last place.

This differs from the domestic net enrollment metric of 49.3% (which uses different age brackets and definitions), but the OECD comparison is sobering regardless of methodology. Colombia is at the bottom of an international ranking for keeping teenagers in school. That is the structural challenge that underlies every other number in this analysis.

## 11. 2025–2026 Turning Points: The Reforms That Change Everything {#2025updates}

### The Law 30 Reform: December 2025

As discussed in the funding section, the unanimous December 2025 reform to Law 30 is not just a budget adjustment. It is a structural shift in how the Colombian state thinks about public universities.

For decades, public universities enrolled more students every year — often without a corresponding increase in real resources — because their budgets grew at the pace of consumer inflation rather than actual operating costs. The gap between what universities needed and what they received was papered over with underpaid adjunct faculty, overcrowded classrooms, and deferred maintenance.

The new funding index changes that equation. Whether the political will to sustain it through future administrations holds is a different question — but the December 2025 vote represents the clearest possible signal that higher education funding is now a national political priority, not an afterthought.

### The CRESE Education Framework

The National Development Plan 2022–2026 under President Gustavo Petro introduced a new strategic educational framework: **CRESE education** — standing for citizenship, reconciliation, socio-emotional learning, anti-racist, and climate change education.

This framework, which the administration describes as "resignifying the school day," represents a philosophical pivot from purely academic coverage toward what might be called formación integral — the development of the whole person. It sits in productive tension with PISA-era metrics focused on cognitive scores: a government saying that what matters in education cannot be fully captured by mathematics and reading tests.

### The IV National Decennial Education Plan (2026–2035)

As the current PNDE 2016–2026 concludes, Colombia has launched the construction of its next 10-year blueprint. The participatory process has involved over a million citizens. Key emerging themes include artificial intelligence integration in classrooms, a deepening of the Matrícula Cero funding model, expansion of rural education infrastructure, and a focus on what planners are calling "pedagogical dignity" — ensuring that teachers, particularly in rural and post-conflict zones, have the professional standing, resources, and safety their role demands.

## 12. The Road Ahead: What Colombia's Education Future Looks Like {#future}

There is a generation of Colombians currently in primary school who were born after the 2016 peace deal. They will never have lived through the worst years of the conflict. They are entering a school system that, for the first time in the country's history, offers free university tuition to most of them. They will study a subject called Peace — mandated by law — before they graduate secondary school.

They will also face, unless current trends change dramatically, a system where:
- A quarter of their cohort will reach 24 years old neither working nor studying
- Upper secondary dropout rates remain among the worst in the OECD
- The quality of their school will be partly determined by whether they were born in Bogotá or in a village accessible only by river
- Their Saber 11 score will be shaped not just by their intelligence and effort, but by whether their family could afford a tutor

The Colombian education system is not a story of success or failure. It is a story of genuine, costly, incomplete transformation — of a country that has decided, repeatedly, through constitutions and laws and street protests and legislative votes, that education is worth fighting for.

The December 2025 reform passed unanimously. Zero votes against. In a polarized political environment, that unanimity is its own kind of statement: across party lines, Colombians agree that public education deserves more.

Whether that agreement survives the next budget cycle, the next election, the next crisis, is the question that determines whether Sebastián's children will inherit a system better than the one he enrolled in — or whether they will still be waiting.
