## The Research Behind Why Timed PYQ Practice Actually Moves Your Score

It's easy to treat mock practice as an afterthought — something you squeeze in once the "real" studying is done. Turns out the research says the opposite: taking practice tests is one of the most effective ways to actually learn the material, not just check what you already know.

- **Practice testing beats re-reading and re-watching notes.** In a landmark review of ten learning techniques for the *Psychological Science in the Public Interest* journal, Dunlosky et al. (2013) rated **practice testing** as one of only two techniques with "high utility" for durable learning — well above highlighting, re-reading, or summarizing. A 2021 meta-analysis by Hattie and Donoghue, covering 242 studies and over 169,000 participants, found the same thing: **distributed practice and practice testing are the two most effective learning techniques measured.**
- **The "testing effect" is real and well-replicated.** Roediger and Karpicke's foundational 2006 study showed that actively recalling information under test conditions produces stronger long-term retention than simply re-studying the same material — even when the practice test itself isn't scored.
- **Timed practice reduces exam-day anxiety, it doesn't cause it.** A 2026 quasi-experimental study (Al-Thani & Akour, *International Journal for Research in Education*) found that students who took practice tests showed a significant drop in test anxiety over time — and anxiety was negatively correlated with final exam performance. Separately, research by Agarwal et al. (2014) found that regular low-stakes retrieval practice **reduced exam anxiety and increased student confidence**, rather than adding pressure.

For an exam like CAT — where sections are timed independently, you can't revisit a closed section, and the on-screen palette is unfamiliar the first time you see it — the value isn't just "more questions attempted." It's building enough familiarity with the *format itself* that your working memory on exam day goes entirely toward solving problems, not figuring out where the "Mark for Review" button is.

Timed attempts build your exam temperament. Reading explanations builds actual understanding. You need both — most free "mock test" sites give you neither, just a score at the end and no way to see why an answer was wrong.

Better for a first pass through an unfamiliar topic, or for close reading on DILR sets and RC passages where the *reasoning path* matters more than the final answer.

## A Simple Way to Work Through These Ten Papers

1. **Start with 2021 or 2022 in Study & Understand mode.** No timer. Read every question and its explanation. This builds pattern recognition before speed matters.
2. **Move to full timed attempts for 2023–2025**, negative marking on, in one sitting where possible — 120 minutes, all three sections, no pausing. Treat it like exam day.
3. **Use the topic filter on any paper** to isolate and repeat your weakest topics — Geometry, Number System, or Time & Work in QA, or Logical Reasoning sets in DILR, for instance — without re-running the whole paper.
4. **Review every attempt within 24 hours.** Go through both right and wrong answers using the explanations, not just the ones you missed. Understanding *why* a correct answer worked is what actually transfers to a new question next time.
5. **Save your most recent two papers for the final two weeks before your exam**, timed exactly to your actual CAT slot, to condition your focus for that specific time of day.
